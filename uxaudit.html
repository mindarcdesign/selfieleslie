#253
Implement Product Types with Shared Attributes as Filters Instead of Categories
What's the UX issue?...
What's the UX issue?
During multiple rounds of both desktop and mobile testing, multiple sites had implemented product types as categories or subcategories when, in fact, the product types should have been implemented as filters. This proved critically harmful, as participants were unable to get full lists of all the products of a certain product type; for example, they couldn’t “see all dresses” in a single product list, but rather had to browse dresses by product type — effectively “siloing” users into overly narrow categories. This often has the practical outcome of end users being unable to find a product they wish to purchase — even if the product exists on the site. During testing, this led many participants to abandon sites due to the confusion and limitations introduced by such mismatches.

How to improve the UX?
Implement product types as filters instead of categories when most product attributes (e.g., “brand” and “style”) are the same across the product type. Implement product types as categories instead of filters mainly when most of the product attributes aren’t shared and therefore the product types are mutually exclusive.

#531
Prominently Display the Total Number of List Items or Search Results
What's the UX issue?...
What's the UX issue?
Users typically perform a preliminary evaluation of both category product lists and search results prior to actually inspecting items in detail. One important consideration at this point is the number of items in the list because it helps them judge whether the list is too large, too small, or seems about right.

During Baymard’s large-scale usability testing, whenever the number of items wasn’t available (or simply not noticed) users often neglected to make an initial judgement of the list; e.g., Are these results too broad? Or perhaps they’re overly narrow? To answer those questions, users typically perform a preliminary snap evaluation of the list prior to actually inspecting its items in detail. If the number of items is missing or is hard to see, users lack the information they need to take action on modifying the list.

How to improve the UX?
Show the total number of items in the product list and on search results pages — both above and below the list — and ensure that the size of the number of items make it easy to discern.

#537
Have Hierarchy-Based Breadcrumbs in Category Product Lists on Mobile and Desktop Sites
What's the UX issue?...
What's the UX issue?
Hierarchy-based breadcrumbs — which indicate where a user is in the site hierarchy — fulfill 2 important functions. Firstly, they help users to orient themselves by showing where the category product list is within the site structure. Secondly, they provide users with a convenient and efficient way to navigate up the hierarchy to broaden the scope of the current category product list.

As observed during testing, without clickable hierarchy-based breadcrumbs, users in category product lists may not know where they are within the site structure, and may not be able to quickly widen the scope of the list and see more items.

How to improve the UX?
Provide clickable hierarchy-based breadcrumbs on category pages and in category product lists, and consider including "Sideways Breadcrumbs" as well.

#467
Always Show the Number of Matches for Each Filter Option, and Update Them as Filters Are Applied
What's the UX issue?...
What's the UX issue?
Most sites display numbers alongside filter options that show the number of products that would match that option if selected. During Baymard's testing, on sites that didn’t show the number of matches for each filter option, participants either wasted time applying filters that didn’t narrow the product lists or search results sufficiently, or ran into dead ends when the application of filters led to too few items being shown in the list.

Therefore, not knowing how many matches would correspond to each filter type slows product finding and makes it much harder for users to tailor product lists to their needs.

However, having numbers of matches besides filter options was observed not being enough — they have to be updated each time a filter is chosen. This is especially important when selecting multiple filters. In fact, unless matching values are updated as soon as a filter is chosen, there is no point in showing the numbers at all after users have selected a filter, as what the user is concerned about is the total size of the resulting product list or search results.

How to improve the UX?
Show the number of matches for each filtering option, and live update the numbers as filters are applied or deselected.

#828
Have Both “Hierarchy” and “History” Breadcrumbs on Product Pages on Mobile and Desktop Sites
What's the UX issue?...
What's the UX issue?
Breadcrumbs help users get an overview of where they are on the site, navigate between categories, and return to search results. Baymard’s large-scale usability testing shows that without “Hierarchy” breadcrumbs on the product details page, it’s more difficult for users to understand where they are within the site structure, resulting in an altogether disorienting browsing experience. Not keeping users apprised of their current location is observed to increase the chance they will, at some point, feel lost and inhibits their ability to navigate confidently.

The absence of “Hierarchy” breadcrumbs also makes it challenging for users to continue their product browsing from the product details page because they’re unable to navigate up the hierarchy easily. They often either have to make drastic scope jumps (e.g., select a new top-level category, perform a new search, etc.), or remain stuck at the product details page searching for an escape route.

Furthermore, users were repeatedly observed getting stuck when sites only featured one type of breadcrumb and not both “Hierarchy” and “History” breadcrumbs.

How to improve the UX?
Always provide both “Hierarchy” and “History” breadcrumbs on product pages.

#416
Provide Text Fields for Numeric Filtering Values
What's the UX issue?...
What's the UX issue?
Numeric product attributes such as weight, dimensions, and price are often key factors when users are choosing among products. However, three main issues were observed during Baymard’s testing to affect users trying to filter product lists or search results by numeric attributes: unsuitable predefined numeric filter ranges, single-value numeric filters, and range sliders.

For predefined ranges, the issue is that users are likely to always see irrelevant results because they can’t select specific values. As a result, the product list or search results will contain too many items, and users will have to expend needless time and effort trying to find products that suit their needs.

For single-value numeric range filters, the issue is that users will have to select multiple values. This process of individually selecting individual numeric options greatly frustrated test participants and most lost patience before having selected the entire range.

Range sliders proved difficult to control, with a few participants being unable to correctly define a suitable range. As a result, the critical task to define a suitable price range became needlessly challenging.

How to improve the UX?
For numeric filtering values, provide both predefined values and text fields.

What's the UX issue?

#1191
Have a “Sales” or “Deals” Filter Type
What's the UX issue?...
What's the UX issue?
Many users browse product lists and search results to see if any items of interest are available at a discounted price — as one participant said during testing, “I always shop the sales first”.

However, on sites that have sale items available but that don’t have a way to isolate them, it’s significantly more difficult to find discounted items. As observed during testing, users looking for bargains will have to scan through product lists or search results that contain both full-price and discounted items, looking for labels or other indications that some products are discounted.

Even if the “On Sale” labels are clear, the presence of nonsale products clutters the list, making it harder to compare the discounted items because the nonsale items get in the way. Furthermore, if “On Sale” labels in list items are not easy to discern, product finding is even slower as users have to first work out which items are indeed discounted.

How to improve the UX?
Provide a “Sale” or “Deal” filter type, and ensure that it is always visible in the filter interface (e.g., don’t hide it in a “Price” filter type).

What's the UX issue?

How to improve the UX?

What's the UX issue?

#783
Product Image Overlays Should Use 80-100% of the Page Width
What's the UX issue?...
What's the UX issue?
Employing a full-screen overlay allows sites to provide images in the largest format possible, which was shown to be helpful to users who were seeking product information via images in Baymard’s large-scale usability testing.

However, when using a full-screen overlay be aware that most users will assume that the browser “Back” button will take them back to the default product page. If the “Back” button doesn’t take them back to the product page from a product image overlay users will be disoriented. For example, if they’ve arrived from an external source and landed directly on the product page, then clicking “Back” will take them off the site entirely and return them to wherever they were previously.

How to improve the UX?
Allow users to close the image gallery overlay by clicking the browser back button. Consider using the HTML5 History API’s url-rewrite, which allows a site to invoke a URL change without a page reload, meaning the site can align the browser history and back button behavior to match user expectations and close a product image overlay.

What's the UX issue?...
What's the UX issue?
Providing cross-sells on the product details page saves users time and effort when searching for alternative or supplementary products. However, without adequate information, it can be challenging for users to evaluate the suitability and compatibility of cross-sell recommendations.

Therefore, it's important to offer a consistent level of basic product details for both alternative and supplementary cross-sell recommendations to give users intuitive and easy access to other products of potential interest.

How to improve the UX?
Provide the product information that's currently missing in the cross-sell recommendations. It's key to provide a high-quality thumbnail, title, price, user ratings average, brand, and the number of product variations or stock status, if relevant.
